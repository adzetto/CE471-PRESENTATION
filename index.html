What is Hydrokinetic Energy?

  • Definition: Energy derived from the motion of water (kinetic energy) rather than its potential energy (head).
  • Sources:
    • River streams (without dams)
    • Tidal currents
    • Ocean currents
    • Man-made channels
  • Analogy: "Underwater Wind Turbines" - utilizing water density instead of air.
CE471 Group

Why Hydrokinetic Energy?

Key Advantages
  • High Density: Water is $\approx 830$ times denser than air.
  • Predictability: Tides and river flows are highly predictable compared to wind/solar.
  • Minimal Infrastructure: No need for massive dams or reservoirs (low civil works footprint).
  • Base Load Potential: Continuous power generation in rivers.

Working Principle

Hydrokinetic devices capture energy from the flow velocity.

Based on Bernoulli's Principle, but primarily utilizing the dynamic pressure component.

$$ P = \frac{1}{2} \rho A v^3 C_p $$
  • $P$: Power output (Watts)
  • $\rho$: Water density ($1000 \text{ kg/m}^3$)
  • $A$: Swept area of the turbine ($m^2$)
  • $v$: Flow velocity ($m/s$)
  • $C_p$: Power coefficient (Betz limit $\approx 0.59$)
CE471 Group

The Cubic Relation

Power is proportional to the cube of velocity ($P \propto v^3$).

  • A small increase in velocity yields a large increase in power.
  • Example: Doubling the velocity ($v \to 2v$) increases power by factor of 8.
Implication for Civil Engineers
Site selection is critical. Finding "hotspots" with high velocity is more important than maximizing area.

Turbine Classifications

Axial Flow

  • Horizontal axis
  • Resembles wind turbines
  • High efficiency
  • Requires alignment mechanism

Cross-Flow

  • Vertical or Horizontal axis
  • Axis perpendicular to flow
  • Can accept flow from any direction (Vertical)
  • Robust design

Civil Engineering: Site Selection

The role of the Civil Engineer is crucial in resource assessment.

  • Bathymetry: Mapping the river/seabed floor.
  • ADCP Surveys: Measuring velocity profiles ($v(z)$).
  • Geotechnical: Soil properties for anchoring.
  • Constraints: Navigation channels, protected areas.

Foundation & Anchoring Systems

How do we keep the turbine in place against strong currents?

Types of Foundations
  1. Gravity Base: Concrete mass (friction holds it).
  2. Monopile: Steel pile driven into seabed (like offshore wind).
  3. Mooring Lines: Floating turbines anchored by cables and drag anchors.

Water Resources: Hydraulic Impact

Extracting energy removes momentum from the flow.

  • Wake Effect: Velocity deficit behind the turbine.
  • Backwater Effect: Slight increase in upstream water level (usually negligible for HK).
  • Sediment Transport: Changes in velocity can affect scour and deposition patterns.

Modeling with CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics) is essential.

Environmental Considerations

  • Fish Interaction: Strike risk vs. avoidance.
    • Turbines rotate slowly compared to ship propellers.
  • Acoustics: Noise impact on marine mammals.
  • EMF: Electromagnetic fields from subsea cables.
  • Water Quality: Generally no pollution, but hydraulic changes matter.

Engineering Challenges

Harsh Environment
  • Corrosion: Saltwater / bio-fouling.
  • Debris: Logs and ice in rivers.
  • Turbulence: Fatigue loading on blades.
  • Grid Connection: Underwater cabling to shore.
  • Maintenance: Access is difficult and expensive.
CE471 Group

Future Outlook

  • Cost Reduction: Learning curves similar to wind/solar.
  • Hybrid Systems: Combining with bridge piers or existing infrastructure.
  • River Applications: Remote communities (replacing diesel).
  • Role of Civil Engineers: Designing resilient infrastructure for these dynamic loads.

Summary

  • Hydrokinetic energy is a promising renewable source.
  • It utilizes the kinetic energy of water ($P \propto v^3$).
  • Civil Engineers play a key role in:
    • Resource Assessment
    • Foundation Design
    • Environmental Impact Mitigation
  • Offers a sustainable alternative with minimal civil works compared to dams.
