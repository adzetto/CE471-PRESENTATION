Energy Need: Global Electricity Generation Keeps Rising

  • Global electricity generation rose from 11,778 TWh (1990) to 28,927 TWh (2022).
  • Demand growth pushes grids to add reliable, low-carbon supply.

Source: Ember data via Our World in Data (2024).

What We Have: Hydropower's Share Is Shrinking

  • Hydropower generation increased to 4,321 TWh in 2022.
  • Its global share fell from 18.3% (1990) to 14.9% (2022).

Source: Ember data via Our World in Data (2024).

The First Commercial Hydroelectric Plant (1882)

  • Appleton, Wisconsin: waterwheel to leather belts to dynamo.
  • Powered early electric lighting for local buildings.
  • Lester Pelton later improved turbine efficiency.

Sources: Wikipedia contributors (n.d.-a, n.d.-b).

Figure 1. The first commercial hydroelectric plant in Appleton, Wisconsin (1882).

Pumped Storage and Diverse Global Uses

  • Pumped storage shifts energy by pumping water uphill during surplus power.
  • Acts as grid-scale energy storage for daily and weekly balancing.
  • Run-of-river and tidal barrages expand hydropower with less impoundment.

Sources: Yang & Jackson (2011); Wikipedia contributors (n.d.-c).

Figure 2. Pumped storage as a grid-scale battery with low-impact hydro options.

Massive Modern Arch-Gravity Dam

  • Arch-gravity dams combine weight and arch action to resist water pressure.
  • Kaplan turbines suit high-flow, low-head rivers common in mega-projects.
  • Projects provide power, flood control, and irrigation water.

Source: Wikipedia contributors (n.d.-d).

Figure 3. A modern arch-gravity dam with Kaplan turbine layout.

Introducing Hydrokinetic Energy: Harnessing Flow, Not Fall

Hydrokinetic energy converts the kinetic energy from flowing water (rivers, tides, currents) into electrical power, without requiring large dams to create an elevation difference (head).
  • Sits between traditional hydropower and marine energy.
  • Uses free-flowing water with minimal civil works.
  • Ideal for canals, straits, and run-of-river corridors.

Source: Khan, Iqbal, & Quaicoe (2008).

Figure 4. Hydrokinetic energy: flow-based conversion without dams.

Power From Moving Water

P = 1/2 ρ A v^3 η

  • ρ: water density (about 1000 kg/m^3)
  • A: rotor area
  • v: flow velocity
  • η: overall efficiency

Doubling velocity increases power by 2^3 = 8 times.

Key Takeaways

  • Global electricity demand keeps growing; firm renewables are critical.
  • Hydropower output rose, but its global share has declined.
  • Legacy hydro spans early waterwheels, pumped storage, and mega-dams.
  • Hydrokinetic systems extend hydropower into low-impact, flow-only sites.
